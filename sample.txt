  I am setting down this account at the request of my daughter, who says that if I do not write it, her children will have only rumors and tavern stories to  
  explain how their grandfather came to the imperial city in chains. She is right, though I wish she were not.                                                
                                                                                                                                                              
  I was a cloth merchant in those days, neither the richest nor the poorest in the city, but comfortable enough to own my own warehouse and keep four         
  apprentices. I had traded under the city's charter for twenty years. My father had traded under it before me. We understood ourselves to be free citizens of
   the empire, protected by our charter and answerable to no lord but the emperor, and to him only in matters of war and treaty. This understanding, as it
  turned out, was worth precisely as much as the conclave chose to honor it, which is to say nothing at all.

  When word came that the conclave had demanded the dissolution of the free cities, we did not believe it at first. The cities were the empire's engines. Half
   the goods that moved along the coast passed through our warehouses. The notion that we should be handed over to whatever petty lord claimed the land
  beneath our docks seemed so obviously ruinous that we assumed cooler heads would prevail. We were wrong about that, though right about the ruin — it simply
  fell on different heads than we expected.

  The city council voted to refuse dissolution. I supported this, as did nearly every merchant I knew. We thought the emperor would intervene on our behalf,
  or at least negotiate some compromise. We had always understood the emperor to be our protector against exactly this kind of noble overreach. Instead, we
  heard nothing for six weeks, and then the fleet appeared in the harbor.

  I will not dramatize what followed. There was no battle. We had no army, only dock guards and hired watchmen. The imperial marines came ashore at dawn, and
  by noon the council members were in irons. By evening, the marines were working from lists. Every guild master, every major merchant, every shipowner —
  anyone whose name appeared in the city's commercial registry above a certain threshold of assessed wealth. I was not important enough to be taken on the
  first day. They came for me on the third.

  My wife wept. My children did not understand. I was permitted to bring nothing. A marine sergeant, not unkind, told me that I should not expect to see my
  warehouse again and that I should make whatever arrangements I could for my household before morning. I spent that night writing letters to my brother, who
  had married into a farming family inland and was beneath the notice of the lists.

  The voyage to the imperial city took eleven days. We were held below decks, which was miserable, but we were fed adequately and no one was beaten. The
  officers seemed embarrassed by the whole business, or perhaps merely bored. One of them, when pressed by a guild master who demanded to know the charges,
  said only that we were traitors to the empire and that our sentence would be pronounced upon arrival.

  The sentencing was conducted in the great square before the palace. We stood in rows — merchants, councilors, shipwrights, goldsmiths, alchemists, the
  entire commercial class of six cities, filthy from the ships and blinking in the sun. The emperor's chancellor read the judgment. Treason against the
  empire. Slavery in perpetuity, ourselves and our descendants, the emperor's property forevermore. A woman beside me fainted. Several men shouted. Most of us
   simply stood, too stunned or too exhausted to react.

  What happened next, I did not understand for months. We were assigned quarters. Not cells — quarters. Small and crowded, yes, but in actual buildings, not
  dungeons. Within a week, imperial clerks appeared with inventories. Every item seized from our warehouses and workshops had been catalogued. We were asked
  to verify the lists and sign for the return of our goods. I remember staring at the clerk, a young gnome with ink-stained fingers, and asking him whether
  this was a joke. He looked at me with complete sincerity and said, "The emperor requires productive slaves, not idle ones. You will resume your trades."

  And so we did. What else could we do? We were slaves who owned our own stock, worked their own trades, and governed themselves through councils that looked
  remarkably like the ones we had lost. It took me the better part of a year to understand that this was not mercy but strategy, and another year beyond that
  to realize that we were, in our chains, freer than we had ever been — because now no lord, no conclave, no petty noble with a claim to our dockside could
  touch us. We belonged to the emperor alone, and the emperor, it seemed, had very little interest in telling us what to do.

  My daughter was born here, in the imperial city, in the domain. She has never known another home. She calls herself a slave with the same tone her mother
  once used to call herself a free citizen — that is to say, with pride and a certain defiance. I find this strange and wonderful and sad, all at once.

  I do not know whether the emperor planned this from the beginning. I have heard clever men argue it both ways. What I know is that I was a free merchant who
   lost everything, and then a slave who got it back, and that my granddaughter will inherit a trading house that pays no tariffs to any lord in the empire.
  If this is slavery, it is a peculiar kind. But then, this is a peculiar empire.
